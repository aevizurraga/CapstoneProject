Peru's Fishmeal Industry

Fishmeal is a product that is used to feed farm animals and some marine animals. It is mainly made from anchovies or other small fish. It's popularity around the world is due to it's high levels of protein and availability. For years now, Peru has led all countries in the production and exportation of fishmeal. In the fishing industry, fish are caught for two distinct purposes. They are caught for direct human consumption (canned, frozen, fresh) or for indirect human consumption (fishmeal, fish oil). About 83% of all fish caught in Peru in 2008 were for indirect human consumption which totaled more than 6 million metric tons.[1] The vast majority of fishmeal production comes from Chimbote, a city north of Lima with the biggest port in the world. Although the fishmeal industry has created jobs for many people and contributes millions of dollars to the Peruvian economy, it has had some negative effects. Due to the many factories that have been built around the city, residents of Chimbote have seen increased levels of pollution. Once a tourist attraction, the beaches are now filled with garbage and dirty water. If the fishmeal industry wants to survive, it must learn to adapt to a changing world.

Peru

Peru is a nation located in South America. It is known for its cuisine and culture. It is also one of the most popular tourist destinations in the world. People from all over visit Cuzco, the capital of the Inca Empire. Peru has access to the Pacific Ocean to the West of it. It has about 1,500 miles of coastline, making fishing a very popular industry.

Chimbote

Chimbote is a small fishing city, located in the district of Santa. Chimbote is about 250 miles north of Peru's capital, Lima. Even though Chimbote is a small fishing city, it is considered the world's biggest fishing port, in terms of fish caught. [citation] It is also one of the main producers of fishmeal in Peru. The fishmeal industry drastically altered Chimbote during the 20th century. Transforming from a place fo a about 30,000 people, visited by tourists due to its beautiful and clean beaches, to a city of about 265,100 people that largely depends on the fishmeal industry for its income.

Fishmeal Production

Peru is the world's largest exporter of fishmeal. It is estimated that Peru, in 2021, will export about 1,100,000 metric tons of fishmeal to the world. The next closest nation, Chile, is estimated to export only about 210,000 metric tons.

One of the main negative impacts of fishmeal production on the environment is the waste of water. It is estimated that to transport 1 ton of fishmeal to the factories, about two to three tons of water is needed.This water is then wasted because it is contaminated and cannot be used for anything else.

In 2005, Peru removed from the ocean about 8,628,000 tons of anchovies for the indirect consumption of humans. If we estimate how much water was wasted to transport the anchovies to the factories, it comes out to about 17 to 24 million tons of water wasted.

Employment is a sector that could be greatly improved by the reduction of the importance of the fishmeal industry. The chart to the left shows that the quantities of fishmeal that is exported does not have an impact on the income of the people living in these areas. While the average monthly income for Ancash, the department where Chimbote is located, has increased consistently with Peru's average, the amount of fishmeal exported has varied greatly during those years.

The strong variations of fishmeal exported each year also affects fishermen and factory workers that produce fishmeal. As seen below, the quantities of fishmeal that is exported by Peru has been greatly affected by natural disasters and quotas imposed by the Peruvian governemnt, affecting employment for the people working in these sectors.
Peru, for years, has dealt with malnourishment. Malnourishment is defined as the "lack of proper nutrition, caused by not having enough to eat, not eating enough of the right things, or being unable to use the food that one does eat." This might seem odd to some since Peru has one of the worlds best cuisines. It is also home to some of the most nutritious grains and vegetables in the world such as potatoes, maca, quinoa, and kiwicha.

Even with all of these foods available inside the country, there are still countless children and adults that suffer from malnutrition. Anchovies, one of the most nutritious fish available in the sea, could be used to feed many across Peru. Norway, a nation that also has an abundance of anchovies in their oceans, uses the majority of their fish landings for the direct consumption of humans. They also have a much lower percentage of population living under malnourishment at 2.5%.
The table on the left side displays the amount of fishmeal that is exported from Peru and the percentage of the Peruvian population considered undernouished. There is a pattern that shows a decrease in percentage of undernouished people when fishmeal exports decrease. As well as an increase in percentage of undernouished people when fishmeal exports increase. While there is not enough evidence to conclude that decreasing the amount of fishmeal exported will result in a decrease in the undernourished population, this relationship should be studied further.

Almost all of the anchovies that are fished out in Peru are reserved for fishmeal. Since 2013, the proportion of anchovies reserved for fishmeal does not under 96%. For Norway, in 2007, the last year for which the data is available, only about 13% of their fish landings are used towards producing fish oil and fishmeal. Peru actually has a yearly quota on the number of anchovies that can be used for direct human consumption. While there have been many proposals to use anchovies to adequately feed many people in Peru, the Peruvian government has failed to implement a plan to use anchovies to reduce the undernourished population.
